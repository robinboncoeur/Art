### 2. Translations of Leviticus

The Translations of Leviticus are clear.

*ARGUMENT FLAWS*:

My argument in the OP is that the translations may be inaccurate, even if the untranslated words are unchanged. This argument is supported by…

The Septuagint Leviticus 18:22 reads: “καὶ μετὰ ἄρσενος οὐ κοιμηθήσῃ κοίτην γυναικός· βδέλυγμα γάρ ἐστιν” literally translates as "and with a male you shall not lie a woman's bed/lying; for it is an abomination."

George Hollenback argued that the Greek translation addressed grammatically the receptive party in male-male sex, not the insertive party, making it more narrow than a blanket condemnation of homosexuality as a whole.

This LXX text then becomes the basis for Paul's allusions in 1 Cor 6:9 and 1 Tim 1:10, where he creates the compound arsenokoitai ("men-bedders.") Scholars argue that this Pauline term has been mistranslated into English as "homosexuals" only since the mid-twentieth century.

Dispute how the Hebrew consonants should be be rendered.

The standard English translation "You shall not lie with a male as with a woman" inserts both as and with, which have no equivalent in Hebrew. The literal Hebrew is: wĕʾet-zākār lōʾ tiškab miškĕbê ʾiššâ tôʿēbâ hīʾ literally translates to "and male you shall not lie lyings-of woman; abomination it."

K. Renato Lings (Love Lost in Translation, 2013; Theology & Sexuality article, 2009) makes the central case that the comparative reading is a translator's gloss, not what the Hebrew syntax requires.

Mark Preston Stone (2022 survey in Currents in Biblical Research) summarizes that "all translations are freely interpretive in their understanding of miškevē ʾiššâ."

The rare word miškevē (the plural construct miškevē - literally "lyings of" - appears in the entire Hebrew Bible only in Lev 18:22, Lev 20:13, and Genesis 49:4. Genesis 49:4 refers to Reuben's incest with Bilhah, his father's concubine.

The form also appears, as noted, in 1QSa I:10 among the Dead Sea Scrolls. The argument runs: the rare word “miškevē “ found only in incest-related contexts probably carries an incestuous connotation in Leviticus too, again implying strongly the ban is against incestuous sex or child abuse, not homosexual sex between two adults.

Lings, following David Tabb Stewart (Ph.D. dissertation 2000, Queer Bible Commentary 2006), and supported in part by Jacob Milgrom's monumental Leviticus commentary (Anchor Bible 3A, 2000, p. 1786), reads Lev 18:22 as: "And with a male, sexual relations of an incestuous nature should be just as abominable to you as they are with a female." This reading the verse closes the chapter's incest list with a "catch-all" rule that the male versions of all the previous female incest prohibitions also apply.

Bruce Wells (On the Beds of a Woman: The Leviticus Texts on Same-Sex Relations Reconsidered, in Sexuality and Law in the Torah, T&T Clark, 2020), argues that miškab in the verb-plus-adverbial-accusative construction means a location, not a manner.

He surveys all eleven other uses of the verb šākab with an adverbial accusative in the Hebrew Bible and finds that in eight, the accusative clearly indicates location (e.g., 2 Sam 4:5, 11:9, 12:16; Ps 88:6; Ruth 3:8, 14). In the remaining three texts (Lev 15:18, 19:20; Num 5:13), where it refer to a sexual manner, the phrase šikbat zeraʿ ("lying of seed," i.e., seminal emission) is used, a phrase clearly available and yet not chosen for use in Lev 18:22 and 20:13.

Wells concludes the verses prohibit sex with a man who is already attached to a woman's sexual domain (i.e., the woman's husband or her young male dependents) making the prohibition a form of adultery or invasion of household, not a universal ban on male same-sex intercourse.

Leviticus 20:13 says, wəʾîš ʾăšer yiškab ʾet-zākār "and a man (ish) who lies with a male (zachar)…"

Ish in Biblical Hebrew normally denotes an adult man, while zachar is the broader word for any male including male children, male animals, or even a male infant.

Nowhere else in Leviticus 18 or 20 does the text use zachar for the prohibited partner; every other prohibited partner is described as a woman or female.

Steven Greenberg (Wrestling with God and Men, 2004), argues that the verse may originally have addressed the specific Greco-Roman cultural practice of using sex as a male domination technique — not all consensual adult male relationships. He argues that Lev. 18:22 should not be read as a blanket condemnation of all male-male intimacy or all gay relationships, but that the verse is focused on a specific penetrative act when bound up with domination, humiliation, or violence.

Philo of Alexandria (1st century CE) read the verse this way, and Luther's German translation rendered zachar as Knaben (boys): "Du sollst nicht beim Knaben liegen wie beim Weibe."

The reason most modern English translations don't reflect this may be that translation choices ossified early as a result of Jerome's Latin Vulgate which translated zachar as the gender-neutral masculus. LXX then glossed it as arsen ("male") and the pederasty-specific reading was lost in transmission. Tyndale, the Geneva Bible, and the KJV all followed the Vulgate/LXX pattern, and modern English translations (NIV, ESV, NRSV) inherit this reading.

ק (nashaq) Kiss: The same word used for familial and romantic kisses.
For the kiss of greeting between relatives (Jacob and Esau, Genesis 33:4; Laban and Jacob, Genesis 29:13)
For the kiss of farewell (Naomi and her daughters-in-law, Ruth 1:9,14)
For the erotic kiss in the Song of Songs 1:2 ("Let him kiss me with the kisses of his mouth")
ד (hesed) — "Steadfast Love / Loyalty"
The primary word used for God's faithful love toward Israel
Used in the covenant context of Ruth and Naomi (Ruth 1:8, 3:10)
Deeply tied to berit (including the love of marriage.) You enact hesed within a covenant relationship
When David discusses that the love of Johnathon was more wonderful than the love of women.
נִפְלְאַתָה (nifleta) "wonderful" or "surpassing": from the root pala, meaning to be extraordinary, beyond comprehension, miraculous. It is the same root used for God doing wonders and miracles. David is saying Jonathan's love was beyond ordinary reckoning.
me'ahavat nashim, "More than the love of women": uses the standard word ahavat (from ahav) and nashim (women). David is making an explicit, direct comparison himself, and declaring Jonathan's love superior. He is not saying it was different in kind — he is placing both on the same scale and saying one exceeded the other.
The word "brother" (אָחִי, achi): achi was commonly used in the ancient Near East in both fraternal and covenantal/treaty contexts, and notably in love poetry. In the Song of Songs, the male lover calls his beloved achoti ("my sister") repeatedly — it was a term of intimate endearment, not only biological relation.
These ambiguities (used both within and without romantic intent) in the language make it easy for people who (for whatever reason) feel the need to deny the obviousness romantic component of the relationship.
