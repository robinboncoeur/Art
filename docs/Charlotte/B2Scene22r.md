The unmistable aroma of shepherd's pie eased something in my shoulders as I pushed the door open. The hinge complained and I reminded myself to give it a bit of attention that weekend.

Charlotte's back faced me in the kitchenette. In fact, all I saw was curved jeans and steam from the oven.

"Hi."

She straightened.

"Hey, petal," she said cheerily. "Dinner won't be long. Can I get you anything."

I stared at her, my face flat. Her brows rose a little but she said nothing, just pursed her lips and turned back to the stove.

I continued to look at her for a moment.

Finally, I went into the bedroom and sank down on the bed, my eyes a bit out of focus.

*I love that girl!*

*Why*?

Those thoughts had been at the centre of everything that afternoon. I accomplished tasks by rote: I'm sure Mara noticed---her look was a bit sharp when she left. Mara always notices things, especially when someone is not one-hundred percent in the task.

I closed my eyes.

And opened them again---there was a sound at the door. Charlotte was peeking round the corner.

"Dinner's ready?"

My smile was thin.

"I'll go wash up."

Charlotte was no chef, but her simple meals were always tasty. I had contemplated having a glass of Sauvignon Blanc but decided against it. Not having alcohol mid-week. I finally leaned back in my chair.

"You've been rather quiet," I said.

"I thought you sort of needed a bit of quiet at the moment."

I gave a tiny nod, fixing the flaky crust with a concentrated stare, listening to the solemn tick of the kitchen clock.

I finally glanced up at her. She was looking at me with her usual mixture of 'not-quite-certain' and adoration. I smiled, a bit more openly now. And saw her shoulders visibly ease.

"You know I love you, don't you?" I said, my voice soft.

Her look warmed, and her shoulders eased more.

"I know."

"And you know me enough to know that I over-analyse everything."

She suppressed a smile and made as if to speak.

I held up my hand.

"Hear me out. You know I saw your interaction with your dad that afternoon last week." A small cloud crossed her face. "I watched a man lose... everything." 

Charlotte's eyes cooled.

"You can't lose what you never---"

"I agree," I cut in. "But that's not the point. He *thought* he had the basic structure of an ideal family. A home. A wife." I paused. "A son."

Charlotte waited. A certain tension had returned.

"Come to find out, the home became just a pile of bricks and mortar when the wife and child no longer lived there." I pursed my lips. "His whole security system evaporated because of one enormous mistake: taking what was the best in his life for granted."

I rocked slightly in my chair, my hands between my thighs.

"Of course, he'd lost everything a long time ago. And *that* was what he was confronted with when he recognised you. I could  see it in his eyes."

I licked my lips.

"Okay, he's an entitled member of the patriarchy. That's a given. But." 

I stilled. My hands trembled a little. Finally, I looked up to meet Charlotte's wide-eyed gaze.

"Anyone can grow used to things being done for them. You start to put it down to nature, not work. The trivial tasks done carefully go unrecognised because they feel trivial. It's a---slippery slope."

For a long beat, neither of us spoke. Finally, I leaned forward, putting my elbows on the table and my head in my hands.

"I love you, Charlotte." Her face remain expressionless, her stare unwavering. "I want to know why."

Her gaze faltered, and she looked at her half-eaten dinner.

"Because you like my cooking?" she offered timidly.

Charlotte wore that same innocent, hopeful 'she-has-the-answers' look I'd found so intriguing in the girls' loo. I reached out and placed my hand on her cheek.

"You are so lovely." 

Her look softened slightly.

"Here's the thing, Charlotte. I love you, genuinely love you, but also, I realise that I have really come to depend on you. I guess I didn't realise that when I told you that you were best at being a wife, that I would come to *need* that wife. Need *you*!" 

Charlotte shook her head slightly.

"And that bothers you?"

I shrugged.

"I know it seems weird now for me to question the whole 'wife' idea, but when I saw the emptiness in your dad's eyes I realised we were two different pieces of cloth looking at the same seam."

"You're nothing like my dad."

"I didn't say I was. We're only similar in that we both need that seam. Your mum provided that for him, and you provide me with the same. Your dad confused need with longing, dependency with love. I don't want to make the same mistake."

Charlotte grimaced, almost a smile.

"You won't."

"And how do you know?"

"You're not male. You're not entitled."

"So, you think women can't be entitled?"

"Well, *also* because you're thinking about this. I doubt my dad had any of those thoughts at all."

"I only *had* those thoughts because of seeing his face."

Charlotte huffed, but then, her eyes lit up.

"How do you think my mum feels about Sarah?"

I frowned.

"I fail to see the relevance."

"You told me back then, that I was to take on the role the way my mum did."

"Then, I should be thinking about how Sarah feels about your mum."

"Yeah, or that."

"Wait. No, you said it the other way around. Why?"

"Because what we---meaning: me and my Mum---think counts too."

"Of course! But---"

"Do you think---" she cut in, "that we can't be guilty of taking a person for granted? Just because we're infrastructure..."

"It's not the same."

"Except it is. Expectations and routine make us less able to see changes when they happen, especially when those changes are unexpected. Mum held the home together, kept everything running, but she saw a story in her head, not me. Expectations and the routine. I think she's still not come to terms with Charlotte."

I drew my lips together with a slight frown.

*She had a point.*

"I want to try something," Charlotte said. "Something we do every day. Or at least three times a week."

"I'm listening."
